## Section 2 — Scenario Brief

> This is presented to the candidate at the start of the simulation, below the scenario introduction video. It is available throughout the simulation for reference.

It is early January. You have just joined Nexus Bank as an Associate Product Manager on a newly formed squad. The timing is not coincidental — in December, Nexus Bank ran a high-profile Christmas advertising campaign positioning itself as the go-to bank for ambitious startups. The response was overwhelming. Within two weeks, the customer service team logged over 1,400 inbound enquiries from founders, early-stage companies, and accelerator graduates asking about business transaction accounts.

The problem is simple and urgent: there is no product. There is interest, but no account that meaningfully serves startup needs. The CEO has now formally commissioned a cross-functional group to build one, and you are the Associate Product Manager responsible for shaping what it becomes. Your squad includes engineers, a UX designer, a compliance lead, and a data analyst. You have been given three months to deliver a product recommendation to the executive team.

You quickly discover that the internal team has no shared understanding of what startups actually need from a bank. Some stakeholders want to replicate a standard SME account with a lower fee. Others want to build deep integrations with accounting tools like Xero and QuickBooks. The Head of Commercial Banking insists that due diligence and compliance requirements cannot be relaxed. Meanwhile, two fintech competitors have launched dedicated startup accounts in the last six months and are gaining ground fast.

**Your Challenge:** Define the product strategy for this new startup account. Starting with research, you must identify what the market actually needs, how the competition is positioned, and what Nexus Bank can realistically build that will win. You have three prompts to work through. Each one tests a different part of your strategic thinking. Write clearly, think like a PM, and show your working.

Before you can define what the Nexus Bank startup account will offer, you need to understand the market. Describe the research process you would use to identify what startups actually need from a business bank account. Who would you speak to, what questions would you ask, and how would you validate your findings before using them to shape the product? Be specific about your methods and explain how you would prioritise the insights you gather.

- **Word guidance:** 200 to 400 words

## Section 4 — Evaluation Rubric

> Rubric criteria are specific to this scenario. Score each criterion: **1 = Weak, 2 = Competent, 3 = Strong**. Maximum score per prompt: 9. Overall maximum: 27.

### Prompt 1 Rubric — Market Discovery

**What this tests:** Whether the candidate can design a structured discovery process and move from vague curiosity to validated insight. Strong candidates will look inside the organisation before going outside.

| Criterion                                 | Weak (1)                                                                                                                                     | Competent (2)                                                                                                                                                   | Strong (3)                                                    |
| ----------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Research Method Selection**             | Lists generic methods such as surveys or interviews with no rationale for why they suit a startup audience or Nexus Bank's specific context. | Identifies two or more appropriate methods such as jobs-to-be-done interviews or competitor account reviews and briefly explains why they are appropriate here. | _(Score 3 implies exceeding Competent across all dimensions)_ |
| **Stakeholder and Source Targeting**      | Refers only to customers or founders without specifying which stage of startup, sector, or need. Does not mention internal sources.          | Identifies a range of startup types or stages and mentions at least one non-obvious source such as accelerators, incubators, or the bank's inbound enquiry log. |                                                               |
| **Insight Validation and Prioritisation** | Assumes hearing a need once from a few people is sufficient to act on, or offers no method for filtering or ranking what they discover.      | Mentions triangulating findings across sources or using a framework to prioritise needs by frequency and severity.                                              |                                                               |

---

### Prompt 2 Rubric — Competitive Landscape

**What this tests:** Whether the candidate can translate competitive intelligence into product positioning decisions, not just produce a feature list comparison.

| Criterion                            | Weak (1)                                                                                                                                    | Competent (2)                                                                                                                                        | Strong (3) |
| ------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------- |
| **Competitive Analysis Depth**       | Describes competitors vaguely or relies on surface-level features without referencing what startups value about those features.             | Identifies specific features offered by Starling or Tide — such as fast onboarding, API access, or no monthly fees — and maps them to startup needs. |            |
| **Nexus Bank Differentiation Logic** | Claims Nexus Bank can win on brand or trust alone without linking those advantages to specific startup pain points or unmet needs.          | Identifies one genuine Nexus Bank advantage — such as established credit lines or regulatory credibility — and connects it to a startup need.        |            |
| **Strategic Application**            | Treats competitive analysis as a standalone exercise and does not connect findings back to what the product should actually do differently. | Uses the competitive analysis to suggest two or three positioning choices for the Nexus Bank account.                                                |            |

---

### Prompt 3 Rubric — Product Strategy Definition

**What this tests:** Whether the candidate can translate discovery and competitive thinking into a clear, defensible product strategy that acknowledges real-world constraints.

| Criterion                            | Weak (1)                                                                                                                              | Competent (2)                                                                                                                                                          | Strong (3) |
| ------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------- |
| **Feature Prioritisation Rationale** | Lists features without explaining why they were chosen, or selects features disconnected from validated startup needs.                | Selects two or three features with a brief justification referencing either market research findings or competitive gaps.                                              |            |
| **Constraint Navigation**            | Ignores the compliance and commercial tension, or dismisses it as a process issue without engaging with the real trade-off.           | Acknowledges the tension and suggests a plausible approach — such as tiered onboarding — that attempts to balance startup expectations with Nexus Bank's requirements. |            |
| **Success Metrics Definition**       | Defines success in vague terms such as growth, satisfaction, or happy customers with no targets, timeframes, or measurement approach. | Names two or three specific metrics — such as accounts opened, 90-day retention, or NPS — with a six-month horizon.                                                    |            |

## Section 5 — Example Responses

> Use these to calibrate AI evaluation scoring and to test your evaluation prompt before candidates use the simulation. Run each example through Claude with the rubric and confirm scores align with the expected levels below.

### Prompt 1 — Market Discovery

#### Strong Response (expected score: 8 to 9 out of 9)

My first move would be to not design anything. Before touching a feature list, I'd want to spend two weeks in discovery — and I'd start with the data we already have. Nexus Bank received over 1,400 enquiries after that campaign. That's a goldmine. I'd ask the customer service team to categorise those enquiries by the type of question asked: is it about fees? About speed of setup? About specific integrations? That segmentation alone would give me early signal on what is actually driving the interest.

Alongside that, I'd recruit 15 to 20 startup founders for jobs-to-be-done interviews — not to ask what features they want, but to understand what problems they are actually trying to solve. I'd deliberately target a mix: pre-revenue founders, companies that have just raised a seed round, and a few who tried to open a Nexus Bank account and gave up. That last group is the most valuable.

I'd also speak to three or four accelerator programme managers — Seedcamp, Techstars UK, and the like. They have a portfolio view of what their cohorts consistently struggle with when banking, which is a different kind of signal to an individual founder's experience.

Once I have raw insight, I'd synthesise using an affinity map and then score themes by frequency and severity. Something that nine founders mention as a weekly problem beats something three mentioned once. I'd turn the top five themes into "how might we" statements and bring those to the squad as the starting point for feature ideation — not a requirements doc, because we haven't earned that certainty yet.

#### Weak Response (expected score: 2 to 3 out of 9)

To understand market needs, I would conduct customer research using surveys and focus groups. I would ask current Nexus Bank customers what features they find most useful and then see if those features would also be useful to startups. I would also look at industry reports and market analysis documents to understand what startups are looking for.

I would also hold a workshop with the internal team to understand what they think customers want. Then I would create a list of features based on the research and rank them by how many customers asked for them. This would help us understand the market and prioritise the most important features.

Once I had the research completed, I would present the findings to the leadership team and we would make a decision on which features to build. The key is to make sure we talk to as many people as possible before starting development so that we build the right product.

---

### Prompt 2 — Competitive Landscape

#### Strong Response (expected score: 8 to 9 out of 9)

Starling and Tide have moved fast, and I'd be fooling myself to say we can beat them on their own terms. Starling's startup account is well reviewed for its clean onboarding — you can open an account in about 10 minutes — and its integration with accounting tools like FreeAgent. Tide is strong on microbusiness and sole trader accounts with no monthly fee, and it has built a good reputation with very early-stage founders who need something simple and cheap.

But there are visible gaps. Neither can offer what a startup needs at Series A or beyond — credit facilities, structured lending, access to FX, or a banking relationship that a VC or grant committee will take seriously. There is also a trust gap: founders raising institutional money or applying for Innovate UK grants often find that their fintech account raises eyebrows. That is Nexus Bank's territory.

My positioning recommendation would be to target the graduation moment — startups that have passed ideation and are now raising, hiring, and dealing with enterprise clients. We would not try to win the sole trader who wants a free account. We would position Nexus Bank as the bank that scales with you: serious enough for investors, flexible enough for a startup, and credible enough when the stakes get real.

#### Weak Response (expected score: 2 to 3 out of 9)

The main competitors in this space are Starling Bank and Tide. They are both digital-first banks that offer business accounts with low fees. Starling has a good app and Tide is popular with freelancers. They both offer mobile banking and integrations with other tools.

Nexus Bank can compete with these companies by offering similar features but with the added benefit of being a trusted high street bank. Startups might prefer Nexus Bank because of its reputation and because it has physical branches. We could also offer better customer service than the challenger banks.

I think the gap in the market is customer service and physical banking support. If Nexus Bank can offer these things alongside the digital features of the challenger banks, we can win the startup market.

---

### Prompt 3 — Product Strategy Definition

#### Strong Response (expected score: 8 to 9 out of 9)

For the first release, I would prioritise three features: a fast digital onboarding flow targeting account open in under 72 hours, a business account with built-in Xero and QuickBooks integration, and access to a dedicated startup relationship manager for accounts crossing a certain transaction threshold. These three because they directly address the top-ranked discovery themes, they are competitively differentiated versus fintechs' self-serve-only model, and they are technically feasible within Nexus Bank's existing infrastructure.

On the compliance tension: I would not try to remove KYC requirements, because that is both illegal and the wrong argument to make. Instead, I would propose a risk-tiered onboarding model. Tier one covers lower-risk applicants — UK-registered companies under two years old, founder-led, no complex ownership structure — who go through a streamlined digital-first journey. Tier two triggers additional review for more complex ownership structures. I would need to work through this design with the compliance lead, and I would make it clear that compliance is a co-designer, not a gatekeeper. That framing matters internally.

Success at six months looks like this: 2,000 accounts opened, 70% 90-day retention (meaning the account is actively used, not just open), and an NPS above 40 from startup customers. I would also track one strategic metric: number of account holders who secure external funding within six months of opening — because that is the signal that we are banking the right companies, not just signing up anyone who applies.

#### Weak Response (expected score: 2 to 3 out of 9)

For the first release, I would prioritise features like a mobile app, online banking, and good customer support. Startups need these things to manage their money effectively. I would also add integrations with popular tools and make the account easy to open.

The compliance issue is important and we would need to work with the relevant teams to make sure we meet all regulations. This might slow things down but it is necessary.

Success would mean more startups opening accounts and being happy with the service. I would measure this through customer satisfaction surveys and by tracking the number of new accounts opened each month.

### 7.6 Submission Type Handling

**Typed responses (Prompt 1):**

- Capture text from textarea input
- Validate minimum 50 words before allowing submission
- Pass raw text directly to Claude evaluation prompt
- Store raw text in responses JSONB field

**File upload (Prompts 2 and 3 upload option):**

- Accept PDF and Word (.docx) only
- Maximum file size 10MB
- Store file in Supabase Storage under `/simulation-uploads/[attempt_id]/`
- Extract text server side before evaluation:
  - PDF: use `pdf-parse` library
  - Word: use `mammoth` library
- Pass extracted text to Claude evaluation prompt
- Store both file storage URL and extracted text in responses JSONB field

**URL submission (Prompt 3 URL option):**

- Validate input is a correctly formatted URL
- Require typed rationale field to be completed (minimum 50 words)
- Attempt to fetch page content server side using `fetch()`
- If fetch succeeds: combine fetched content with typed rationale and pass both to Claude evaluation
- If fetch fails (private link, auth required, or timeout): pass typed rationale only to Claude evaluation and set a flag in the attempt record for coach review
- Store URL, fetch success status, and typed rationale in responses JSONB field
- On coach dashboard: show URL as a clickable link and flag any attempts where fetch failed

### 7.8 Certifier API Integration

Trigger the Certifier API immediately after evaluation completes and verdict is Pass or above (55% or higher).

**Trigger condition:** `overall_percentage >= 55`

**API call:**

```javascript
const response = await fetch("https://api.certifier.io/v1/credentials", {
  method: "POST",
  headers: {
    Authorization: `Bearer ${process.env.CERTIFIER_API_KEY}`,
    "Content-Type": "application/json",
  },
  body: JSON.stringify({
    recipient: {
      name: attempt.candidate_name,
      email: attempt.candidate_email,
    },
    credential: {
      name: "Career Bridge Verified: Product Strategy — Financial Services",
      description: `Awarded for completing the Nexus Bank Product Strategy Simulation with a score of ${attempt.overall_percentage}% (${attempt.verdict})`,
      issued_on: new Date().toISOString(),
    },
  }),
});
```

Store the returned credential ID in `attempts.certifier_credential_id`.

If Certifier API fails, log the error and retry once after 60 seconds. If it fails again, flag for manual issuance. Do not block the results page on Certifier success.

**Critical notes for developers:**

- **Next.js 16 breaking change:** `params` and `searchParams` in layouts, pages, and route handlers are now **async**. Always use `await props.params`. Run `npx next typegen` to generate typed route helpers (`PageProps`, `LayoutProps`, `RouteContext`).
- **Tailwind v4 — CSS-first:** There is no `tailwind.config.js`. All theme tokens go in the `@theme inline {}` block inside `app/globals.css`. The single `@import "tailwindcss"` directive replaces the old `@tailwind base/components/utilities` lines. Do not change `postcss.config.mjs`.
- **shadcn/ui — canary required:** The stable release does not support Tailwind v4. Use `npx shadcn@canary init` and `npx shadcn@canary add <component>`.
- **Supabase client split:** Use `createBrowserClient` inside Client Components (`'use client'`). Use `createServerClient` inside Server Components, Server Actions, and Route Handlers.
- **AI SDK — server only:** Import `@anthropic-ai/sdk` exclusively in `/api/evaluate`. Never import it in client-side code. Never expose `ANTHROPIC_API_KEY` to the browser.
- **Plyr:** Wrap in a `'use client'` component. Load its CSS with `import 'plyr/dist/plyr.css'` inside that same component.
- **pdf-parse and mammoth:** Server-side only. Import inside API routes or Server Actions only.
- **Certifier API:** Direct `fetch()` HTTP calls — no SDK. See section 7.8 for the exact request shape.
- **Vercel deployment:** Set all environment variables in the Vercel dashboard. Never hardcode them in `next.config.ts`.
